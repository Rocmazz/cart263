# Project 1 Response

## 1. Simple Drawing App

Link: https://yatsukki.github.io/cart263/Project1/draw.html

The first project I looked at was **Simple Drawing App**, which felt like a nostalgic recreation of an old Windows XP style drawing program. What stood out to me right away was the aesthetic. The interface felt playful and very intentional, especially with the retro internet references, the cat gif, and even the Game Boy Advance SP title screen reference. It did not just feel like a drawing tool, but more like a small interactive environment.

What I found especially compelling was how immersive it felt. The ability to draw with the mouse, change colors, erase, and even move the window around made it feel much more complete than I expected. That small detail of being able to drag the window really helped sell the illusion of using an actual operating system instead of just interacting with a web page. I also noticed what looked like a sprite sheet or at least custom icon work, which added a lot to the interface. It showed that the code was not only functional, but also supporting a very clear visual identity.

## 2. I Spy

Link: https://aydanmcgrail.github.io/cart263/Project1_aydan/

The second project, **I Spy**, felt much more game-like. In this project, planes move across the screen and the player has to crush them using different methods. What I found most interesting was the variety of controls. Instead of relying on only one interaction, the project let the player use the mouse hand as well as keyboard-triggered actions like coins or forks. That made the experience feel chaotic in a fun way.

What surprised me most was how much energy the game had despite being based on a simple concept. The different speeds of the planes added tension, and the fact that the player could technically spam different attack methods at once made it feel a little messy, but in a way that matched the tone of the game. It created a kind of over-the-top interaction that made the project memorable. From a coding perspective, I imagine there was a lot happening in terms of movement, collision, timing, and multiple input systems, which made it feel mechanically richer than it first appears.

## 3. You Are Being Optimized

Link: https://namikoko.github.io/project1/

The project that surprised me the most was **You Are Being Optimized**. Compared to the other two, this one felt much more intense and conceptually ambitious. The experience was immediately immersive, and it honestly felt like the kind of project that makes you stop and think, “how do you even make this?” The minimalist black-and-white visual style worked really well because it made everything feel cold, controlled, and unsettling.

The most striking part for me was the camera interaction, especially the way it transformed the user’s image into a letter-based or ASCII-like effect. That alone made the piece feel much more advanced and unsettling. Beyond the visual effect, the project was compelling because it used interaction to create discomfort rather than just entertainment. Instead of making the user feel in control, it made them feel watched, processed, and overwhelmed. That made the experience much more memorable for me. It also showed how code can be used not only to make something functional or fun, but also to communicate an idea in a powerful way.
