# Project II Response

https://3andman.github.io/263-REPO/project2/start.html  

The first project I looked at was **High Stakes**, and right away it reminded me of Balatro. I’m a big fan of that game, so it immediately caught my attention. What I liked most is that it actually does what it’s supposed to do. It feels like a simplified web-based version of Balatro that you can just pick up and play. Obviously it doesn’t have all the deeper mechanics and complexity, but the core idea is there and it works well in this format.

One thing I appreciated was how the Joker system was handled. In Balatro, Jokers can get pretty complex, but here they’re introduced in a much more manageable way, where you choose between upgrades after rounds. It feels like a smart decision to simplify something that is normally a big part of the game, while still keeping the idea intact. From a coding perspective, I imagine a lot of the work is happening behind the scenes, especially with detecting poker hands, calculating scores, and managing randomness. The interface itself is simple, but the logic is doing most of the heavy lifting, which I found interesting.

https://xp30n.github.io/SAVE-THE-INTERNET/  

The second project, **SAVE-THE-INTERNET**, was honestly pretty overwhelming, but in a way that felt intentional. The whole experience is chaotic, with pop-ups, ads, and things constantly interrupting you. It actually reminded me a bit of the Simple Drawing App project from before because of that Windows XP style aesthetic, which I really liked. That kind of retro desktop look just works really well here and adds a lot to the experience.

At the same time, the project is genuinely frustrating to use, but I think that’s the point. Instead of making something fun or smooth, it leans into annoyance, which reflects what the internet can feel like now. The pop-ups multiplying when you try to close them was probably the most noticeable part for me. It creates this feeling of losing control over the interface. Also, the whole cat and camera interaction stood out because it felt a bit invasive and unpredictable. I’m not entirely sure how everything works technically, but it seems like there’s a lot going on with APIs, randomness, and different systems interacting at once. Overall, it’s messy on purpose, and that’s what makes it effective.

https://nounoune666.github.io/cart263/projects/projectTwo/  

The last project, **Avatar Sim**, was probably the one that surprised me the most. I genuinely didn’t expect something like this to be possible in JavaScript for a school project. The whole 3D environment feels really polished, and being able to move around and click on objects that lead to actual links adds a level of interactivity that feels very intentional.

What stood out to me is how everything is clickable and responds in a way that encourages exploration. It’s not really a game with a clear goal, but more like a space you navigate through. The use of 3D models, animations, and lighting makes it feel closer to something you’d see in a game engine rather than a browser project. From a technical side, I imagine this relies heavily on Three.js, especially for things like raycasting to detect clicks and interactions. Even without fully understanding how it’s built, it’s clear that a lot of work went into integrating models and making the scene feel alive. Overall, it feels very ambitious and stands out because of that.
